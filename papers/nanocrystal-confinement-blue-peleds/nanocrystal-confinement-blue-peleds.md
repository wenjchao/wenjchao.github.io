**Abstract.** Metal halide perovskites have emerged as promising semiconductors for light-emitting diodes (LEDs) owing to their excellent luminescence properties^1^. However, their performance remains limited, primarily owing to the inherent contradiction between 'high crystallinity' and 'small size' in the in situ synthesis of perovskite nanocrystals on substrates. Here we report efficient blue perovskite LEDs (PeLEDs) achieved via in situ polymerization-driven nanocrystal confinement to synthesize perovskite films composed of high-quality nanocrystals. The in situ-formed polymer network imposes nanoscale spatial constraints during perovskite nanocrystal growth, enabling nanocrystals with small sizes and a high photoluminescence quantum yield of 83%. Furthermore, polymerizable monomers with sufficient coordination sites allow a prolonged lattice rearrangement of perovskite clusters, promoting the crystallinity of the nanocrystals. The synthesized perovskite nanocrystals are utilized in the fabrication of PeLEDs, resulting in an external quantum efficiency of 21.8% at 491 nm, which is among the highest performances in blue PeLEDs. This work simultaneously controls the thermal dynamics of perovskite crystallization and organic ligand reactions, which helps to advance understanding of the effect of ligand engineering on nanocrystal synthesis, benefiting the development of efficient PeLEDs and other optoelectronic technologies.

Metal halide perovskites are promising candidates for next-generation light-emitting materials owing to their tunable bandgaps, high colour purity and low-cost solution processability^1,2^. Recently, perovskite light-emitting diodes (PeLEDs) have undergone rapid developments, with the external quantum efficiency (EQE) exceeding 30% for green and red emissions^3–6^. Nevertheless, there is still a clear gap between PeLEDs and organic LEDs as well as metal chalcogenide-based quantum-dot LEDs^7–12^, especially for the blue-emitting counterparts. To improve the performance of blue PeLEDs, perovskites with high crystallinity and nanoscale grain sizes are required. High crystallinity with low defect density suppresses non-radiative recombination losses and material degradation^13^, whereas small grains enhance radiative efficiency through charge confinement and limited diffusion^14,15^.

Despite extensive studies on perovskite crystallization kinetics, it is still an inherent challenge to simultaneously achieve perovskite nanocrystals with high crystallinity and nanoscale confinement through in situ synthesis on substrates. Current strategies, including solvent engineering^16,17^, thermal annealing^18,19^ and additive engineering^20^, primarily improve crystallinity by retarding nucleation and promoting crystal growth. These approaches contribute significantly to the device stability^13^, but typically yield micrometre-scale grains that limit emission efficiency. Meanwhile, perovskite nanocrystals grown in situ on substrates generally suffer from low monodispersity, defective surfaces and poor crystallinity, which collectively degrade their luminescence^21^. As a result, this long-standing trade-off between crystallinity and size confinement has limited the full potential of perovskite nanocrystals in PeLED applications. Therefore, it is necessary to develop a simple, controllable and universal approach that enables precise control of both crystallinity and nanoscale confinement in perovskite nanocrystals for optoelectronics.

Here we propose a facile in situ polymerization strategy to produce perovskite nanocrystals with both high crystallinity and confined sizes. The polymer network formed in situ from oligo(ethylene glycol) methyl ether acrylate (OEGA) dynamically constrains nanocrystal overgrowth during crystallization, reducing the grain size from over 250 nm to 11 nm. This facilitates carrier confinement and intergranular coupling. Owing to the strong coordination affinity, OEGA interacts effectively with perovskite precursors, moderating the initial rapid growth of perovskite clusters and thereby improving crystallinity. The fully confined nanocrystals exhibit a cubic phase at room temperature with decreased octahedral distortions, substantially mitigating non-radiative losses caused by electron–phonon coupling. Accordingly, blue PeLEDs realize a recorded EQE of 21.8% and prolonged operational stability of 69.4 min. This work demonstrates a feasible in situ nanocrystal confinement approach that advances understanding of the effect of ligand engineering on perovskite nanocrystal synthesis, benefiting the development of efficient blue PeLEDs and related optoelectronic technologies.

## In situ nanocrystal confinement strategy

Inspired by colloidal perovskite nanocrystal synthesis, we find that the ligand structure has a crucial role in crystallization processes, influencing nanocrystal size, monodispersity, morphology and surface states^20,22^. For in situ synthesis on substrates, ideal ligands should provide continuous confinement throughout the perovskite crystallization process, and have a strong binding affinity to their framework to prevent rapid cluster aggregation. This promotes crystal rearrangement and ordered lattice alignments, yielding high-quality, small perovskite nanocrystals with reduced defects^23,24^. However, directly introducing conventional organic ligands can not meet both requirements, prompting our development of an in situ polymerization strategy using polymerizable monomers. Effective monomers require: (1) linear rather than cyclic configurations for minimal steric hindrance and effective nanocrystal encapsulation^25^; and (2) sufficient coordination sites for strong binding towards the perovskite lattice^26^.

Cs~0.7~EA~0.3~PbBr~3~ (EA, ethylamine) blue-emitting perovskites are selected as a typical example to evaluate this in situ-confinement strategy and its potential application in LEDs. The pristine film, prepared via the one-step anti-solvent approach^27^ (Fig. 1a), exhibits large crystal gains of 270 ± 88 nm (Fig. 1b and Supplementary Fig. 1a). Attempts are made to directly introduce ligands into precursor solutions, including non-polymerizable passivating molecules and polymers (with carbonyl or ether groups as electron donors) (Extended Data Fig. 1 and Supplementary Figs. 2 and 3). Non-polymerizable ligands such as 2-methoxyethyl acetate (N-MOA) and dimethyl malonate fail to reduce the grain size and instead yield larger perovskite grains (514 ± 120 nm and 504 ± 125 nm), probably owing to their short-chain structures limiting spatial confinement. Moreover, when high-molecular-weight polymers (methoxy polyethylene glycol (MPEG) and poly(acrylamide) (PAA) are incorporated directly, they tend to form amorphous aggregates with poor perovskite lattice affinity, yielding grains averaging 202 ± 44 nm. Impressively, polymerizable monomers such as 2-methoxyethyl acrylate (P-MOA) significantly reduce the perovskite grain size to 52 ± 16 nm (Fig. 1c and Supplementary Fig. 1b). This trend extends to other monomers such as 2-aminoethyl methacrylate (AEM), N,N′-methylene bis(acrylamide) and trimethylolpropane triacrylate, resulting in average nanocrystal sizes of 40–60 nm (Supplementary Fig. 4). These monomers with unsaturated vinyl groups enable easy polymerization with free radical initiators at low temperatures^28,29^, ensuring in situ polymerization during nanocrystal growth. The consistent size reduction across diverse monomer structures highlights the central role of the in situ polymerization reaction in modulating perovskite crystallization.

Mechanistic studies further reveal that as the number-average molecular weight (M~n~) of the monomer increases from 120 (P-MOA) to 480 (OEGA) and 1,000 (poly(ethylene glycol) methyl ether acrylate, MPEG-MAA), the monomers increasingly constrain the nanocrystals to 9 ± 1 nm (Fig. 1c–e and Supplementary Fig. 1b–d). In contrast, an excessive molecular weight of 5,000 induces ligand self-aggregation, causing nanocrystal size heterogeneity (Fig. 1f and Supplementary Fig. 1e). Furthermore, controlled increases in ligand concentration can also effectively reduce nanocrystal sizes (Supplementary Figs. 5 and 6). This implies that the in situ synthesis of controllable nanocrystal sizes can be achieved by adjusting both the chain length and content of polymerizable monomers.

Among the tested ligands, OEGA provides an optimal balance between confinement and charge transport in perovskite nanocrystals. OEGA (1%) reduces the grain size to 11 ± 3 nm (Fig. 1d) and forms a thin coating layer via strong coordination, as evidenced by electron energy loss spectra (Fig. 1g). Low-dose high-resolution transmission electron microscopy analysis reveals a ligand-induced structural phase transition. Fast Fourier transform analysis of pristine perovskites (Fig. 1h) shows diffraction spots indexed to the orthorhombic (\(\gamma\)) phase along the [223] and [332] zone axis. In contrast, OEGA nanocrystals exhibit cubic (\(\alpha\)) symmetry across multiple crystallographic orientations (Fig. 1i and Extended Data Fig. 2c–f), indicating an orthorhombic-to-cubic phase transition. The transition is also verified by X-ray diffraction measurements, showing that peak evolves from orthorhombic (Pnma) to cubic symmetry (Pm\(\bar{3}\)m) with increasing OEGA content (Fig. 1j and Extended Data Fig. 2a,b). Notably, the OEGA-treated films show significantly enhanced diffraction intensity, reflecting their improved crystallinity.

To elucidate the impact of polymerizable monomers, we investigated their evolution and interaction with perovskites. Proton nuclear magnetic resonance (^1^H NMR) confirms the thermal polymerization of OEGA (Fig. 2a). After annealing, vinyl proton signals at 5.9–6.4 ppm disappear completely, signifying polymerization of vinyl groups and formation of a poly(oligo (ethylene glycol) acrylate) (POEGA) network. Electron paramagnetic resonance further verifies the absence of residual radicals in the final film (Supplementary Fig. 7 and Supplementary Table 1). X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (Fig. 2b) reveals that both Pb 4f~5/2~ (138.1 eV) and Pb 4f~7/2~ (143.0 eV) peaks shift towards lower binding energies after OEGA incorporation, demonstrating reduced charge on Pb^2+^ ions through OEGA coordination. A similar negative shift is also observed for the Br 3d~3/2~ and Br 3d~5/2~ peaks (Supplementary Fig. 8a). This interaction promotes the formation of OEGA coating layers throughout the crystallization process, as evidenced by electron energy loss spectroscopy analysis (Fig. 1g), facilitating defect passivation and grain stabilization.

To investigate the effects of in situ polymerization on the entire crystallization process of perovskites, we conducted in situ ultraviolet–visible absorption and photoluminescence analyses (Fig. 2c,d). Pristine perovskites show simultaneous emergence of absorption and photoluminescence peaks upon anti-solvent treatment, indicating rapid cluster aggregation and growth. In contrast, OEGA-modified samples show delayed emission onset with gradual redshift and intensity enhancement, suggesting suppressed initial cluster aggregation and improved lattice relaxation. During subsequent annealing, pristine films show pronounced thermal photoluminescence quenching (Supplementary Fig. 8b), whereas OEGA perovskites achieve enhanced final absorption and photoluminescence intensity, consistent with improved crystallinity and confined nanocrystal sizes.

The growth dynamics are further visualized by in situ liquid-phase electron microscopy imaging of precursor solutions (Extended Data Fig. 3). Pristine precursors form sol–gel intermediates instantaneously under low-dose electron irradiation (5 e^−^ (Å^2^ s)^−1^), rapidly grow along the nanopipette tip, and ultimately grow into uncontrollable large aggregates. In contrast, OEGA- and 2,2′-azobis(2-methylpropionitrile) (AIBN)-modified precursors show no detectable nucleation for over 10 min under identical conditions. Increasing the electron dose rate to 12 e^−^ (Å^2^ s^−1^) induces solvent extraction and local supersaturation^30^, mimicking the perovskite spin-coating process. This triggers the formation of numerous nanoscale particles (approximately 10 nm) that remain stable over time, contrasting with pristine systems. These observations align with the in situ optical measurements (Fig. 2c,d), demonstrating that OEGA delays nucleation of perovskite precursors and subsequently stabilizes nanocrystal size through polymer-network confinement. Comparatively, non-polymerizable ligand N-MOA slows nanocrystal growth but fails to confine the size owing to the absence of a rigid network, yielding larger particle sizes. Preformed polymer PAA shows negligible nanocrystal growth restriction, reflecting its static and weak interactions with precursors. These contrasts demonstrate that the in situ polymerization process is critical for size regulation, providing key insights into ligand-mediated growth control for nanocrystal engineering.

To clarify the mechanism, density functional theory calculations (Supplementary Note 1) are performed to compare the thermodynamics of OEGA and solvent (dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO)) coordination with lead bromide (PbBr~2~). The carbonyl (0.74 eV) and ether (0.67 eV) groups of OEGA show slightly weaker binding energies with PbBr~2~ (Supplementary Fig. 9 and Supplementary Table 2) than DMSO (Supplementary Fig. 10 and Supplementary Table 3), yielding a minimal binding-energy difference (\(\Delta E_b\approx 0.1\,\text{eV}\)) for one-site OEGA adsorption to replace DMSO (Supplementary Fig. 11). However, such an exchange increases entropy owing to the release of free DMSO molecules, yielding a favourable Gibbs free energy (\(\Delta G=-0.66\,\text{eV}\); Supplementary Tables 4 and 5), which is expected to further decrease upon polymerization. The thermodynamic stabilization arises from multiple O–Pb coordination of OEGA, adopting a crown ether-like configuration (Supplementary Fig. 9) known to slow crystallization and improve crystal quality^31–33^. OEGA also binds nanocrystal surfaces at multiple sites (Supplementary Fig. 12), while its in situ polymerization provides crystallization modulation, as discussed below.

Molecular dynamics simulations of polymerization reactions (Supplementary Note 2, Supplementary Fig. 13 and Supplementary Table 6) reveal that monomer structure critically influences polymerization efficiency and growth modulation. Small monomers such as P-MOA polymerize rapidly owing to high densities of reactive termini, whereas their short side chains lack sufficient coordination capacity to restrict crystal evolution (Supplementary Figs. 14 and 15). Medium-length OEGA achieves optimal kinetic synchronization with perovskite crystallization through balanced reactive group densities, whereas its extended side chains offer structural flexibility and considerable sites that facilitate polymer–perovskite interactions for controlled growth (Supplementary Figs. 16 and 17). Large monomers such as MPEG-MAA (M~n~ 1,000 and 5,000) show suppressed polymerization rates owing to self-interactions, limiting their utility in perovskite crystallization. These findings highlight the importance of balancing polymerization dynamics and coordination affinity for crystal engineering.

On the basis of these findings, Figs. 1a and 2e schematically illustrate the crystallization pathway enabled by the in situ polymerization strategy. OEGA incorporation enables an in situ-formed polymer network, which effectively constrains crystal overgrowth during crystallization. The multiple coordination bonds thermodynamically stabilize intermediates, and further suppress rapid perovskite cluster growth while facilitating intergranular coupling. The prolonged relaxation of small clusters promotes ordered lattice formation with reduced defects, leading to enhanced crystallinity^23^. In contrast, pristine films show accelerated phase transformation from intermediates to perovskite during spin-coating, where unregulated cluster aggregation disrupts optimal stacking and generates lattice imperfections^34^. Subsequent annealing further promotes grain coarsening, leading to increased non-radiative recombination losses.

These results establish in situ polymerization as a distinct strategy for confined nanocrystal growth. Although conventional non-polymerizable passivators can improve crystallinity by slowing growth, they often induce excessive grain coarsening. In contrast, the dynamic confinement environment generated by polymerization simultaneously reinforces crystallinity and restricts grain growth, providing a synergistic pathway to enhance radiative recombination.

The phase of lead halide perovskites is governed by [PbBr~6~]^4−^ octahedral distortions, which critically determine optical and electronic properties. Whereas bulk pristine grains adopt the orthorhombic phase, the in situ-confined nanocrystals maintain the high-temperature cubic phase at room temperature. It is demonstrated that, in addition to pressure, temperature and compositions^35^, the physical size critically modulates phase stability. Density functional theory calculations^36^ reveal that the cubic phase has significantly lower surface energy than the tetragonal and orthorhombic phases (Supplementary Note 3 and Supplementary Tables 7 and 8). Therefore, size confinement stabilizes the perovskite cubic phase by increasing the surface-to-volume ratio (Extended Data Fig. 2g,h and Supplementary Table 9), which leads to a reduced critical temperature for the orthorhombic-to-cubic phase transition. Moreover, ligand-induced surface interactions further reinforce this stability^37,38^, enabling the confined nanocrystals in our experiments to maintain the cubic phase as thermodynamically favourable at room temperature.

The in situ-confinement effect drives a phase transition of perovskite nanocrystals from the original orthorhombic to cubic structure, with direct implications for luminescence properties. Temperature-dependent photoluminescence measurements show gradual intensity attenuation and full-width at half-maximum (FWHM) broadening from 100 K to 325 K (Supplementary Fig. 22). Notably, pristine nanocrystals exhibit more severe thermal quenching than their cubic counterparts, even at low temperatures (Fig. 3a), which could be partially attributed to stronger electron–phonon coupling associated with octahedral distortions in the orthorhombic lattice. Quantitative analysis based on the temperature-dependent linewidth broadening^39–41^ reveals that the in situ-confined nanocrystals show markedly reduced longitudinal-optical (LO) phonon energy and electron–phonon coupling strength (\(\gamma_{\mathrm{LO}}=115.5\,\text{meV}\), \(E_{\mathrm{LO}}=28.7\,\text{meV}\)) compared with pristine nanocrystals (\(\gamma_{\mathrm{LO}}=228.0\,\text{meV}\), \(E_{\mathrm{LO}}=39.2\,\text{meV}\); Fig. 3b). This result indicates suppressed lattice vibrational energies in the in situ-confined nanocrystals, consequently reducing non-radiative recombination pathways and enhancing the photoluminescence quantum yield (PLQY).

Low-frequency Raman spectra further elucidate the lattice vibrations in pristine and OEGA perovskites (Fig. 3c). Pristine perovskites exhibit five Raman peaks at 200 K, assigning to octahedral distortions^42^, which broaden and redshift with increasing temperature owing to enhanced thermal vibrations and electron–phonon interactions^42^. In contrast, OEGA perovskites exhibit a broad spectral band with a central peak, reflecting inherent vibrational instabilities from highly anharmonic double-well potentials in the cubic phase^43^. Crucially, OEGA samples maintain spectral stability at 200 K, confirming robust low-temperature phase stability. The significantly reduced Raman intensity in OEGA perovskites indicates suppressed lattice vibrational modes, critically mitigating electron–phonon coupling^44^.

The incorporation of OEGA enhances perovskite luminescence through simultaneous size confinement and improved crystallinity, which promote radiative recombination while suppressing non-radiative losses. OEGA films show nearly doubled photoluminescence intensity and an extended average lifetime of 15.2 ns compared with 7.5 ns for pristine films (Fig. 3d,e and Supplementary Table 10), accompanied by only a slight blueshift (Supplementary Fig. 23). Ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy and Urbach-tail analysis reveal a more ordered energetic landscape after OEGA incorporation (Supplementary Fig. 24a–d). Transient absorption spectra show a single narrow bleach peak (Supplementary Fig. 24e), and the bleach wavelengths keep constant in time, indicating high nanocrystal monodispersity. Structural analyses confirm that PEA predominantly resides at grain boundaries rather than lattice sites, as the substantially higher binding strength of OEGA to the nanocrystal surfaces prevents structural reorganization (Supplementary Fig. 25). This dual modification enhances photoluminescence uniformity and stability of perovskite films, as evidenced by photoluminescence mapping of fresh and aged samples (Supplementary Fig. 26).

On the basis of the optimized OEGA/PEA perovskite films with enhanced stability and PLQYs, PeLEDs were fabricated with the architecture of indium tin oxide/m-PEDOT:PSS/perovskite/TPBi/LiF/Al (Supplementary Fig. 27). PeLEDs incorporating confined perovskite nanocrystals show narrow blue emission at 491 nm with CIE (International Commission on Illumination) coordinates of (0.07, 0.29) and a FWHM of 23 nm (Fig. 4b,g). Incorporation of OEGA/PEA induces a modest increase in current density under high voltage compared with pristine devices (Fig. 4c), accompanied by a substantial enhancement in maximum luminance from approximately 720 cd m^−2^ to 1,925 cd m^−2^ (Fig. 4d). As shown in Fig. 4e, the optimized devices achieve a peak EQE of 21.8%, nearly doubling that of pristine devices (10.7%), ranking among the highest EQEs reported for blue PeLEDs (Supplementary Table 11). A comparison study of devices modified with PEA, OEGA and OEGA/PEA (Supplementary Fig. 28), respectively, reveals that OEGA has the dominant role in performance enhancement. Statistical analysis of 32 devices further confirms good reproducibility, with the average EQE increasing from 9.0% for pristine devices to 19.5% for OEGA/PEA devices (Fig. 4f).

Apart from the high-quality confined nanocrystals, device efficiency also benefits from the optimized surface morphology of OEGA/PEA perovskite films. As evidenced by scanning electron microscopy and atomic force microscopy images (Supplementary Fig. 29), isolated submicrometric particles are uniformly distributed across the surface of OEGA/PEA perovskite films, which probably arise from the aggregation of confined nanocrystals (Extended Data Fig. 4). This morphology facilitates the extraction of lateral-propagating waveguide modes in planar devices, thereby reducing optical losses^45^ and providing a complementary contribution to the device performance (Supplementary Fig. 30).

The OEGA/PEA PeLEDs also show improved spectral and operational stability. As shown in Fig. 4g, both the electroluminescence peak position and FWHM of the OEGA/PEA PeLED remain constant under a bias voltage up to 8 V, in contrast to pristine devices (Supplementary Fig. 31). The operational half lifetime (T~50~) is markedly increased from 10.3 min of the pristine device to 69.4 min of the OEGA/PEA PeLED. To understand this stability enhancement, we tested the thermal conductance (\(\sigma(T)\), where \(\sigma\) is the conductivity and \(T\) is the temperature) of perovskite films to estimate ion-migration activation energies (\(E_a\))^46,47^. Fitting results (Fig. 4h) yield an \(E_a\) of 0.27 eV for pristine films and 0.77 eV for OEGA/PEA films, confirming that the in situ-formed polymer network effectively suppresses ion migration.

## Conclusion

We have developed an in situ-confinement strategy that enables the simultaneous realization of high crystallinity and nanoscale size control in perovskite nanocrystals grown directly on substrates. The dynamically formed polymer network restricts nanocrystal overgrowth, while strong coordination between polymerizable ligands and perovskite clusters prolongs lattice rearrangement, yielding highly crystalline, low-defect nanocrystals without sacrificing charge transport. This study enriches the scope of ligand categories and advances understanding of the effect of ligand engineering on nanocrystal synthesis. By simultaneously controlling the thermal dynamics of perovskite crystallization and organic ligand reactions, it solves the contradiction between the 'high crystallinity' and 'small size' in the in situ synthesis of perovskite nanocrystals on substrates. This method is both simple and universal, with potential for extension to diverse quantum dots owing to similar interactions, advancing high-performance PeLEDs and next-generation optoelectronics.

## Methods

### Materials and reagents

Lead bromide (PbBr~2~), caesium bromide (CsBr, 99.999%), DMSO and poly(sodium 4-styrene-sulfonate) (PSS-Na, M~w~ (weight-average molecular weight) ≈ 70,000) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. Trimethylolpropane triacrylate and MPEG (average M~n~ = 5,000) were purchased from Macklin. Poly(acrylamide) (average M~n~ = 40,000) and 2-methoxyethyl acetate (M-MOA) were purchased from Adamas. Dimethyl malonate was purchased from Bide Pharmatech. 2-Aminoethyl methacrylate was purchased from Konoscience. N,N′-methylene bis(acrylamide) and potassium bromide (KBr) were purchased from Aladdin. TPBi was purchased from Xi'an Polymer Light Technology. Lithium fluoride (LiF, 99.99%) was purchased from Alfa Aesar. P-MOA (Sigma-Aldrich), OEGA (average M~n~ = 480, Sigma-Aldrich) and MPEG-MAA (average M~n~ = 1,000 and 5,000, Macklin) were purified by passing through basic alumina to remove the radical inhibitor before use. AIBN (Sigma-Aldrich, 98%) was recrystallized from methanol. Further adjustments are made to the purchased PEDOT:PSS (AI 4083) aqueous solution to fine-tune its work function and improve infiltration. The modified-PEDOT:PSS solution was a 1:1 volume mixture of a double-diluted PEDOT:PSS aqueous solution and an aqueous solution containing 15 mg ml^−1^ PSS-Na and 3 mg ml^−1^ KBr.

### Materials synthesis

PEABr was synthesized by slowly adding 0.12 mol hydrobromic acid to a solution of 0.1 mol phenethylamine in ethanol with vigorously stirring at 0 °C for 2 h. Subsequently, the solution was concentrated by evaporating the solution at 50 °C, which was washed with ethanol 3 times and then dried under vacuum at 40 °C for 24 h. The final products were sealed in a nitrogen-filled glovebox for future use.

### Perovskite precursor solution preparation

The perovskite precursor solution was prepared by dissolving CsBr (0.1 M), EABr (0.044 M) and PbBr~2~ (0.11 M) into DMSO under continuous stirring overnight. For OEGA/PEA perovskites, OEGA, PEABr (0.03 M) and AIBN (1% of OEGA) were directly added to the precursor solution before use, and the ratio of OEGA refers to the mass ratio between OEGA and PbBr~2~. Before use, the mixture was filtered through a 0.45 µm polytetrafluoroethylene membrane.

### Device fabrication

The indium tin oxide (ITO)-coated glass substrates were sequentially cleaned by sonication in detergent, deionized water, acetone and isopropyl alcohol, and then further treated with ultraviolet-ozone cleaner for 20 min. The m-PEDOT:PSS aqueous solution was spin-coated onto the ITO substrates and annealed at 150 °C for 15 min in ambient air. Then the perovskite films were prepared in a nitrogen-filled glovebox by spin-coating at 3,500 rpm. After spin-coating for 36 s, 450 µl toluene anti-solvent containing 0.2 mg ml^−1^ poly(methyl methacrylate) was dropped onto the film, followed by annealing at 60 °C for 20 min and a subsequent 3 s CF~4~ plasma treatment. Finally, TPBi (40 nm), LiF (1 nm) and Al electrode (100 nm) were deposited sequentially through a thermal evaporator.

### Characterization

The steady-state photoluminescence spectra and time-resolved photoluminescence spectra were recorded on the FLS980 (Edinburgh Instruments) with an excitation at 345 nm. The ultraviolet–visible absorption spectra of the samples were obtained by a ultraviolet–visible diffuse reflectance spectrophotometer (UV–vis DRS, Japan Hitachi UH4150). The broadband femtosecond transient absorption spectra were taken using the Ultrafast System HELIOS TA spectrometer. All the samples were encapsulated with quartz flakes during these optical measurements. The in situ absorbance measurements were tested on a Quartz Flow-Through Semi-Micro Cuvette with a 2 mm slit. The fluorescence microscopy images were tested under a laser scanning confocal microscope (Nikon). The temperature-dependent low-frequency Raman spectra were collected using a home-built microscope. A 633-nm helium–neon laser (N-LHP-925, Newport) was filtered from undesirable amplified spontaneous emission using a volume Bragg grating (VBG) bandpass filter (Optigrate, linewidth 5 cm^−1^). To minimize the Rayleigh scattered light, the collected signal was sent through the VBG reflecting filter and passed through three VBG notch filters (Optigrate, optical density > 3) before being dispersed in the spectrograph. The X-ray diffraction patterns were collected by using a PANalytical X'Pert Pro X-ray powder diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (wavelength λ = 1.54050 Å). To improve the signal-to-noise ratio, the precursor solution concentration was magnified. The ^1^H NMR measurements were recorded on a Bruker-400 MHz NMR. The samples were scraped off after spin-coating and redissolved in the solvent for testing. To improve the signal-to-noise ratio, the OEGA concentration was magnified. The morphology of perovskite films was measured using a scanning electron microscope (S4800) and an atomic force microscope (Bruker). The X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) data were obtained by an Axis Ultra XPS spectrometer (Kratos) with Kα radiation of Al, and operated at 150 W. XPS and ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy were obtained by an Axis Ultra XPS spectrometer (Kratos) with Al Kα radiation and ionizing helium I, respectively. The current density–voltage–luminance curve, EQE and operational stability measurement of our perovskite LEDs and the PLQY measurement were conducted using a commercialized system (LQE-100-EL, Enlitech) equipped with an integrated sphere and a photomultiplier tube, in which the photomultiplier tube was used to measure the low luminance up to 10^−4^ cd m^−2^, ensuring accurate measurement of luminance. The start and stop voltages of the EQE measurement were set at 2.8 V and 6 V, respectively, with a voltage step of 0.1 V, a dwell time of 100 ms and a delay of 100 ms. All the device characterization tests of perovskite LEDs were recorded at room temperature in the nitrogen-filled glovebox for the unencapsulated devices. To confirm the measurement results, third-party laboratory cross-checks were performed.

Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) samples were prepared by spin-coating precursor solutions onto carbon-coated copper grids, followed by thermal annealing under conditions consistent with those used for thin-film fabrication. To evaluate the potential influence of the underlying layers, additional TEM samples were prepared by spin-coating perovskite layers on ITO substrates pre-coated with the respective transport layers, followed by gentle scraping and redispersion in toluene before drop-casting onto copper grids. TEM characterization was carried out using an aberration-corrected microscope (JEOL Grand ARM 300) operated at 300 kV. To minimize beam-induced damage of perovskite nanocrystals, the samples were tested with a pixel detector-based ultralow-dose electron imaging camera (Gatan, K2). Particle size analysis was conducted using Nano Measurer software. Multiple TEM images collected from different regions of the grid and from different samples were used for statistical analysis. The particle size was defined based on the edge length of the cubic nanocrystals, and overlapping or aggregated particles were excluded from the analysis. In total, more than 100 particles were analysed for each sample.

Liquid-phase TEM images were collected with a JEM-2100 Plus (HC) microscope with a Gatan OneviewIS camera operated at 200 kV. Quartz capillaries (outer diameter 1.0 mm, inner diameter 0.7 mm, length 10 cm, Sutter Instrument) of liquid-phase electron microscopy were positioned on a CO~2~-laser-based puller (P-2000, Sutter Instrument). A typical two-line protocol was used to generate nanopipette liquid cells with conical shape: (1) HEAT: 835; FIL: 5; VEL: 30; DEL: 128; PUL: 90, (2) HEAT: 865; FIL: 4; VEL: 25; DEL: 132; PUL: 195. The precursor solution was injected into a quartz capillary with a nanosized conical tip from the open end and left undisturbed for 3 h to allow precursor diffusion to the nano-tip. The tip (0.5–1 mm in length) was cut and sealed with vacuum grease at the open end, then loaded onto a carbon-coated TEM grid to create a nanopipette liquid cell.

The temperature-dependent photoluminescence spectra were recorded on an FLS980 (Edinburgh Instruments) equipped with a liquid-nitrogen-cooled cryostat (Oxford Instruments, OptistatDN-V); a 375-nm laser was used as the excitation source. The extracted FWHM of the photoluminescence spectra are plotted as a function of temperature and fitted with the independent Boson model^39–41^:

$$\Gamma(T)=\Gamma_0+\Gamma_{\mathrm{ac}}+\Gamma_{\mathrm{LO}}+\Gamma_{\mathrm{imp}}=\Gamma_0+\gamma_{\mathrm{ac}}T+\frac{\gamma_{\mathrm{LO}}}{e^{E_{\mathrm{LO}}/k_{\mathrm{B}}T}-1}+\gamma_{\mathrm{imp}}e^{-E_b/k_{\mathrm{B}}T} \quad (1)$$

Here \(\Gamma_0\) is a temperature-independent inhomogeneous broadening coefficient. \(\Gamma_{\mathrm{ac}}\) and \(\Gamma_{\mathrm{LO}}\) are electron–phonon coupling coefficients arising from acoustic (ac) and longitudinal-optical (LO) phonon scattering, with the corresponding coupling strengths of \(\gamma_{\mathrm{ac}}\) and \(\gamma_{\mathrm{LO}}\). \(E_{\mathrm{LO}}\) represents the LO phonon energy. \(\Gamma_{\mathrm{imp}}\) accounts for scattering from ionized impurities with the average binding energy \(E_b\) and the coefficient \(\gamma_{\mathrm{imp}}\), which is exceedingly small and can be neglected in the fitting. \(k_{\mathrm{B}}\) is the Boltzmann constant. As the electron-carrier interaction is dominated by high-energy LO phonons in the high-temperature regime^41,48^, we fit the FWHM data based on LO phonon modes.

Temperature-dependent conductivity measurements were conducted using a Keithley 2400 and the temperature was controlled by a Cryo Industries Liquid Nitrogen Dewars with Lake Shore model 335 cryogenic temperature controller. Perovskite films were fabricated on glass substrates and 80-nm gold electrodes with spacing of 50 µm were subsequently deposited. The ion activation energy (\(E_a\)) can be derived by fitting the conductivity curve with Nernst–Einstein equation:

$$\sigma(T)=\frac{\sigma_0}{T}\exp\!\left(-\frac{E_a}{k_{\mathrm{B}}T}\right) \quad (2)$$

where \(\sigma\) is the conductivity, \(T\) is the temperature, \(E_a\) is the activation energy and \(k_{\mathrm{B}}\) is the Boltzmann constant.

### Computational section

**Density functional theory calculations.** The molecular clusters were investigated using density functional theory (DFT) calculations with ADF^49,50^ using AMS2023^51^. Slater Type Orbitals with TZP basis functions were used for all atoms^52^, treating the lighter atoms (H, C, O and S) with an all-electron basis set, whereas the cores for the heavier atoms were kept frozen (Br 1s–3p and Pb 1s–4d). The Zero Order Regular Approximated (ZORA) Hamiltonian was used to include spin–orbit coupling^53^. Exchange-correlation interactions between the electrons were modelled with the Perdew, Burke and Ernzerhof (PBE) functional^54^ to which we explicitly added dispersive interactions through the Becke-Johnson (BJ) damped DFT-D3 scheme^55^. Structures are visualized using OVITO^56^.

The binding energy of molecules on a perovskite surface and phase-stability calculations were investigated using DFT calculations with the projector-augmented wave method as implemented in the Vienna Ab-initio Simulation Package (VASP)^57–59^. The outermost electrons of H (1s^1^), C (2s^2^2p^2^), N (2s^2^2p^3^), O (2s^2^2p^4^), Br (4s^2^4p^5^), Cs (5s^2^5p^6^6s^1^) and Pb (6s^2^6p^2^) were treated as valence electrons. In all calculations, the plane-wave basis set was expanded to 500 eV with Γ-centred k-meshes^60^. For the binding-energy calculations, we modelled the exchange-correlation interactions using the PBE + D3(BJ) functional^54,55^. The phase stability calculation used the PBEsol functional^61^, and the changes in the bandgap were calculated with the r^2^SCAN meta-GGA functional^62^. The Gibbs free energy was calculated from \(G=E_{\mathrm{ele}}+ZPE+pV+\Delta U_{(0\to T)}-TS\) by VASPKIT^63^, here \(E_{\mathrm{ele}}\) is the electronic energy, ZPE is the zero-point energy, p is the pressure, V is the volume, \(\Delta U_{(0\to T)}\) is the thermal correction to the internal energy from 0 K to temperature T, and S is the entropy.

The structural models for the binding-energy calculations were created from a bulk structure of the cubic phase of CsPbBr~3~, which was optimized with a 6 × 6 × 6 k-mesh. Slabs with a thickness of 6 octahedral layers and with a PbBr~2~-termination were made from 2 × 2 supercells in the lateral dimension with 25 Å of vacuum. The perovskite slabs with the adsorbed molecule were optimized using a 3 × 3 × 1 k-mesh by allowing the ionic positions to change. Models were visualized using OVITO^56^.

To assess the phase stability, we optimized the structures of CsBr and PbBr~2~, the precursors of CsPbBr~3~, as well as the different phases of the perovskite, that is, cubic, tetragonal and orthorhombic. The reciprocal space of the precursors was sampled with an 8 × 8 × 8 and 4 × 7 × 3 k-mesh for CsBr and PbBr~2~, and a 6 × 6 × 6, 4 × 4 × 6 and 4 × 4 × 3 k-mesh was used for the cubic, tetragonal and orthorhombic phase of CsPbBr~3~, respectively. The various compounds were optimized by allowing the ionic positions, cell shape and cell volume to change. The optimized perovskite geometries were then used to create slabs with CsBr or PbBr~2~ termination and a thickness of 8 octahedral layers with 25 Å of vacuum. The reciprocal space of these slabs was sampled using a 6 × 6 × 1, 4 × 4 × 1 and 4 × 4 × 1 k-mesh for the cubic, tetragonal and orthorhombic phases of CsPbBr~3~, respectively. Mixed cation systems were created from the optimized unit cells of the cubic and orthorhombic phases of CsPbBr~3~, replacing Cs^+^ with EA^+^ in a model structure with eight octahedral units. These systems were optimized using a 3 × 3 × 3 and 2 × 4 × 3 k-mesh for the cubic and orthorhombic phases, respectively. During optimization of the ionic positions, cell shape and cell volume were allowed to change.

### Molecular dynamics simulations

Molecular dynamics simulations were done using the General Amber Force Field (GAFF)^64^ as implemented in AMS2023^51^. Model systems were created using PACKMOL^65^. The atom types and bond types in the model systems were determined using the antechamber module^66^ based on data obtained from MOPAC calculations^67^. In the simulations, a time step of \(\Delta t=0.5\,\text{fs}\) was used. The damping constants for the thermostat and barostat were \(\tau_T=100\,\text{fs}\) and \(\tau_p=2500\,\text{fs}\), respectively. The systems were equilibrated with a Berendsen thermostat and Berendsen barostat^68^, whereas the production runs made use of an NHC thermostat^69^ and an MTK barostat^70^. For the barostats, an equal scaling for all dimensions (x, y and z) was used to prevent a collapse of the simulation cell in one of the periodic directions. For both pure solvent systems and polymer–solvent systems and equilibration time of \(t_{eq}=200\,\text{ps}\) was found to be enough to achieve system equilibration. The production runs were carried out for \(t_{prod}=200\,\text{ps}\) and \(t_{prod}=1\,\text{ns}\) for the pure solvent and polymer–solvent systems, respectively. Simulation snapshots are visualized using OVITO^56^.

### Optical simulation

Three-dimensional finite-difference-time-domain simulations were performed to calculate the theoretical outcoupling in planar and patterned PeLEDs. We established a structure of glass (n = 1.5)/ITO (n = 1.8, 110 nm)/m-PEDOT:PSS (n = 1.63, 15 nm)/perovskite (n = 2.10/2.01, 20 nm)/TPBi (n = 1.7, 40 nm)/Al (100 nm). The real part (n) and imaginary part (k) of the refractive index of the m-PEDOT:PSS and perovskite layers were obtained from an ellipsometer and others were from the literature^13^. The nanostructure of the patterned OEGA/PEA films was modelled with the detailed surface morphologies from the scanning electron microscope and atomic force microscope images. Multiple simulations with different polarizations were carried out to simulate the incoherent isotropic light source. The total radiated power (P) was obtained according to the following equation^71^:

$$P=\Theta P_{\parallel}+(1-\Theta)P_{\perp}=\Theta(P_x^s+P_y^s)+(1-\Theta)P_z^s \quad (3)$$

where \(\Theta\) is the horizontal dipole ratio defining the orientation of transition dipole moments, and \(P^s\) represents the radiated power contributed by dipoles polarized along the corresponding spatial direction. For isotropic emitters that possess randomly oriented dipoles with equal probability for all directions, \(\Theta\) equals 2/3.

## Supplementary Note 1: Interaction energies

### Molecular binding energies

To assess the binding strength of a various molecules including the polymers and solvents to PbBr~2~, we calculate the binding energy \(E_b\) as

$$E_b=\frac{E_{\mathrm{complex}}-E_{\mathrm{PbBr}_2}-n_{\mathrm{molecule}}\cdot E_{\mathrm{molecule}}}{n_{\mathrm{molecule}}}$$

where \(E_{\mathrm{complex}}\) is the total energy of the complex in which the molecules are bonded, \(E_{\mathrm{PbBr}_2}\) is the energy of an isolated PbBr~2~ molecule, \(E_{\mathrm{molecule}}\) is the energy of an isolated molecule and \(n_{\mathrm{molecule}}\) is the number of molecules coordinating PbBr~2~ in the complex. For all complexes the geometry was optimized from several starting configurations, the most stable complexes were used in the determination of the binding energies.

### Organic ligands

To probe the interaction strength of the organic ligands with the perovskite precursor, we compute the binding energies for various ligand conformations with PbBr~2~. Comparing the interaction energies of OEGA (n = 9) and a shorter model system (n = 5), we find minimal differences in the binding strength between PbBr~2~ and the organic ligands, highlighting the local nature of the O-Pb bonds. As a result of this, we use the shorter model system to compute the binding energies associated with the various binding modes of the organic ligands. Supplementary Fig. 9 provides an overview of the different binding modes between organic ligand and PbBr~2~, their respective binding energies can be found in Supplementary Table 2. The binding energies indicate that the formation of additional O-Pb bonds between the organic ligands and inorganic PbBr~2~ provide additional stabilization for the molecular complexes that is facilitated by the flexibility of the organic backbone of the ligands.

### Solvent molecules

To investigate the binding energies of DMSO molecules to PbBr~2~, we progressively add more DMSO molecules until the lead atom in PbBr~2~ is fully coordinated (\(n_{\mathrm{DMSO}}\)). In this fully coordinated geometry Pb forms six bonds arranged in an octahedral shell around the Pb in the center: two Br-Pb bonds and four O-Pb bonds. The most stable complexes are shown in Supplementary Fig. 10 with their respective binding energies and the energy difference associated with the adsorption of an additional DMSO molecule (\(\Delta E_{\mathrm{complex}}\)) reported in Supplementary Table 3. The successive adsorption of solvent molecules diminishes the energy gain per molecule, as demonstrated by \(\Delta E_{\mathrm{complex}}\), which is likely the result of repulsive interactions that arise from the packing of solvent molecules around the PbBr~2~ molecule.

### Solvent exchange reaction

The reaction enthalpy for the solvent exchange reaction was modeled by comparing the energies of PbBr~2~ complexes in which PbBr~2~ is completely coordinated by DMSO molecules against complexes in which a part of the PbBr~2~ molecule is coordinated by the organic ligand. Both DMSO and the polymer can bond to PbBr~2~ through O-Pb bonds, as shown in Supplementary Fig. 11. We compute the reaction enthalpy \(\Delta H\) as

$$\Delta H=E_{\mathrm{complex}}^{\mathrm{p}}-E_{\mathrm{complex}}^{\mathrm{s}}$$

with \(E_{\mathrm{complex}}^{\mathrm{p}}\) and \(E_{\mathrm{complex}}^{\mathrm{s}}\) the energies of the molecular complexes in which the organic ligand and DMSO bind PbBr~2~, respectively. In the calculations we used shorter chain lengths (n = 3, 5) than for OEGA used in the nanocrystal synthesis (n = 9), to prevent any reorientations of the organic ligand far away from the PbBr~2~ from influencing the reaction enthalpy of solvent exchange. For both complexes we optimized the cluster geometry using different starting points, using the most stable configurations for both (Supplementary Fig. 11), we find an enthalpy for solvent exchange of +0.12 eV and +0.09 eV for n = 3 and n = 5, respectively.

### Surface adsorption

To assess the ability of OEGA-like molecules to the surface of CsPbBr~3~ nanocrystals, we compute the adsorption energies shorter molecules (n = 1; i.e. P-MOA) to the surface of CsPbBr~3~. We compute this adsorption energy \(E_{\mathrm{ads}}\) as

$$E_{\mathrm{ads}}=E_{\mathrm{complex}}-E_{\mathrm{molecule}}-E_{\mathrm{surface}}$$

where \(E_{\mathrm{complex}}\) is the energy of the molecule bonded to the surface, \(E_{\mathrm{molecule}}\) is the energy of the isolated molecule and \(E_{\mathrm{surface}}\) is the energy of the isolated surface. For the optimized bonded geometry, we only allow the ions in the molecule to relax. The resulting geometries, shown in Supplementary Fig. 12, have adsorption energies that range from −0.53 eV to −0.71 eV. The adsorption energies of a single adsorption site are slightly smaller compared to the binding energies with PbBr~2~ molecules (Supplementary Table 3). However, it is interesting to note that on perovskite surfaces, multiple adsorption sites are available, and the adsorption energy per ligand increases with the number of available sites for O-Pb bonds.

## Supplementary Note 3: Nanocrystal phase stability

### Nanocrystal energy

To identify the most stable phase of perovskite nanocrystals of a specific size, we compare the total energy of the nanocrystals with different phases following the method proposed by Yang and Tan^19^. The total energy of a nanocrystal contains a bulk and a surface contribution, which we calculate per formula unit as

$$E_{\mathrm{NC}}=\Delta_{\mathrm{bulk}}+\Delta_{\mathrm{surf}}.$$

The bulk contribution \(\Delta_{\mathrm{bulk}}\) is the perovskite formation energy per formula unit, which is defined as

$$\Delta_{\mathrm{bulk}}=E_{\mathrm{f}}=E_{\mathrm{CsPbBr}_3}-E_{\mathrm{CsBr}}-E_{\mathrm{PbBr}_2},$$

where \(E_{\mathrm{CsPbBr}_3}\), \(E_{\mathrm{CsBr}}\), and \(E_{\mathrm{PbBr}_2}\) are the total enthalpies of the materials from DFT calculations. Assuming cubic nanocrystals, the energy contribution of the surface \(\Delta_{\mathrm{surf}}\) is written as

$$\Delta_{\mathrm{surf}}=\frac{2d^2\cdot\left(E_{\mathrm{surf},1}+E_{\mathrm{surf},2}+E_{\mathrm{surf},3}\right)}{d^3/V_{\mathrm{unit}}}=\frac{2V_{\mathrm{unit}}\cdot\left(E_{\mathrm{surf},1}+E_{\mathrm{surf},2}+E_{\mathrm{surf},3}\right)}{d},$$

where \(E_{\mathrm{surf},j}\) (j = 1, 2, or 3) is the surface energy, \(d\) is the diameter of the nanocrystal, and \(V_{\mathrm{unit}}\) the unit cell volume of the perovskite phase. The surface energy is calculated as^20^

$$E_{\mathrm{surf},j}=\frac{1}{2A_j}\left(E_{\mathrm{slab},j}-N_{\mathrm{unit}}E_{\mathrm{bulk}}-\sum_i n_i\mu_i\right),$$

where \(A\) is the surface area of the slab, \(E_{\mathrm{slab},j}\) is the total energy of the slab from DFT, \(N_{\mathrm{unit}}\) is the number of formula units in the bulk, \(E_{\mathrm{bulk}}\) is the DFT total energy of the bulk, \(n_i\) is the number of species at the surface, and \(\mu_i\) is the chemical potential of the species at the surface termination. For the cubic phase, all surfaces are identical \(\left(E_{\mathrm{surf},1}=E_{\mathrm{surf},2}=E_{\mathrm{surf},3}\right)\), because of the highly symmetric structure. This similarity is lifted in lower-symmetry phases, such as the tetragonal and orthorhombic phase, with the different surfaces having varying energies.

For stable perovskite phases, the chemical potential \(\mu_i\) must satisfy the following relations^19,21^

$$\mu_{\mathrm{CsBr}}+\mu_{\mathrm{PbBr}_2}=E_{\mathrm{CsPbBr}_3}$$

$$\mu_{\mathrm{CsBr}}\leq E_{\mathrm{CsBr}}$$

$$\mu_{\mathrm{PbBr}_2}\leq E_{\mathrm{PbBr}_2},$$

which prevent CsPbBr~3~ from decomposing into its precursors, i.e. CsBr or PbBr~2~. Depending on the conditions, the chemical potential of the species is determined. In a CsBr-rich environment, \(\mu_{\mathrm{CsBr}}=E_{\mathrm{CsBr}}\) and \(\mu_{\mathrm{PbBr}_2}=E_{\mathrm{CsPbBr}_3}-E_{\mathrm{CsBr}}\). For a PbBr~2~-rich environment, \(\mu_{\mathrm{PbBr}_2}=E_{\mathrm{PbBr}_2}\) and \(\mu_{\mathrm{CsBr}}=E_{\mathrm{CsPbBr}_3}-E_{\mathrm{PbBr}_2}\).

### Phase stability

Using the above, the total energies of perovskite nanocrystals can be determined for various phases, surface terminations, and synthesis conditions. By comparison of the total energies, the most stable nanocrystals can be identified. The parameters used to determine the total energies of the nanocrystals can be found in Supplementary Table 7–8.

For very large nanocrystals (\(d\to\infty\)), the surface contribution becomes negligible, and the total energy of the nanocrystal approximates the formation energy of the bulk, thus predicting the orthorhombic (\(\gamma\)) phase to be most stable. For smaller nanocrystals, the surface contribution starts to play a role. Due to the smaller surface energies of the cubic (\(\alpha\)) phase, the total energy of the orthorhombic and cubic phase starts to compete. Suppose that we grow CsBr-terminated (PbBr~2~-terminated) surfaces in CsBr-rich (PbBr~2~-rich) conditions, we find that for nanocrystals of 1.8 nm (2.8 nm) or smaller the cubic (\(\alpha\)) phase is the most stable, as shown in Extended Data Fig. 2. The tetragonal (\(\beta\)) phase, with its higher bulk energy than the \(\gamma\)-phase and its higher surface energy than the \(\alpha\)-phase, has a higher energy than the other phases and therefore is not the most stable phase for any nanocrystal size. Our calculations predict a smaller nanocrystal size at which the cubic becomes more stable than in the experiments (10 nm in experiments). However, as shown in earlier work, the finite temperature dynamics of metal halide perovskites, specifically close to surfaces, aids the formation of cubic domains^22^. Thus, increasing the critical size below which the cubic phase becomes the most stable phase.

Another factor contributing to the discrepancy found above is the effect of ethylammonium (EA^+^) on CsPbBr~3~. To study this effect, we create model structures of the \(\alpha\)- and \(\gamma\)-phase with EA^+^ replacing Cs^+^. Due to the large size of the EA^+^ cations, the cubic phase exhibits a more significant octahedral tilt, whereas this tilt diminishes in the orthorhombic phase. As such, the energy difference of the \(\alpha\)-phase and \(\gamma\)-phase is smaller for the EA^+^-containing perovskite (Cs~0.75~EA~0.25~PbBr~3~) than for pure CsPbBr~3~ (Supplementary Table 9). Therefore, the energy of \(\alpha\)-Cs~0.75~EA~0.25~PbBr~3~ shifts down compared to that of \(\alpha\)-CsPbBr~3~ (Extended Data Fig. 2), remaining stable for much larger nanocrystal sizes. We propose that the combination of the surface and bulk effects discussed above may account for the relatively large sizes of the cubic phases observed experimentally.

We note that our mixed perovskite model that incorporates EA^+^ does not predict the \(\gamma\)-phase is most stable at any nanocrystal size. This outcome is likely due to the overstabilization of the \(\alpha\)-phase, which may stem from the simplified unit cell used in DFT calculations. Furthermore, changes in surface energies arising from more complex surface chemistry, which were not considered in this study, could further influence phase stability.
